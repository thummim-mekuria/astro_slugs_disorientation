Land Grant Development

The Amah Mutsen are the most recent and widespread ancestral peoples of the land where we live, encompassing the modern counties of San Benito, Monterey, Santa Cruz, Santa Clara, and San Mateo. Historically, there were more than 20 politically distinct peoples. Now, the surviving descendant families of these groups represent the modern tribe. You can donate monthly to land restoration and/or volunteer with the Amah Mutsen land trust.

The University of California was chartered as a land-grant college in 1868. Land-grant universities came about when, in 1862, President Abraham Lincoln signed the Morrill Act, which sold off public domain lands, which had been forcibly seized from Native peoples, to raise funds for colleges across the nation. The Morrill Act worked by essentially turning land taken from tribal nations into seed money for higher education - by design, this is an element of settler colonialism. Ultimately, Native people in California did not receive a single cent for the land sold to fund the University of California‚Äôs endowment. Now, we must address how our university‚Äôs history was founded on Native dispossession and genocide, and how UC continues to benefit from this wealth accumulation today.

Quote: ‚ÄúNative American dispossession was not merely an unfortunate by-product of the establishment of land-grant colleges; rather, the colleges exist only because of a state-sponsored system of Native dispossession.‚Äù [citation] ‚ÄúThe noble and virtuous land-grant mission was founded on distortions, violence, and the ongoing suffering and sacrifice of dispossessed Native peoples.‚Äù [citation]

Funds from California Native land were granted to 32 land-grant universities across the country, including schools in Virginia, Louisiana, and Maine. Some schools have developed programs to redirect continuing income from Morrill Act funds to provide tuition aid to Native students; however, these programs are not widespread and are primarily initiated by the local Native tribes near that school without administrative support from the university. [citation]

UC Davis has put together an initiative called Reimaging the Land-Grant University [website] to compile resources regarding the UC history as a land-grant university and initiatives for making systemic amends to Native peoples.

In 2020, a forum entitled The University of California Land Grab: A Legacy of Profit from Indigenous Land was held online on September 25 and October 23, 2020. The forum was organized by Berkeley staff, graduate students, and faculty, with key input from colleagues at UC Agriculture and Natural Resources, UC Davis, and UC Riverside. A summary of the results of this forum can be found here. One primary recommended action from this forum is for the UC to allocate a percentage of the return on Morrill Act endowment funds to compensate tribes who were dispossessed via the Act. Funds can be used for a wide variety of purposes, from establishment of Native field institutes to tuition waivers for California Indian students.

The Founding of UCSC

UCSC was founded in 1965, when the Regents decided to create three new campuses (including Irvine and San Diego). They felt that the university was becoming too impersonal and bogged down by bureaucracy - as such, UCSC was to be a university in the woods where people could connect with nature and learn more freely. To this end, UCSC was founded as a university with a college system and no grades for students (instead, each student received a ‚Äúnarrative evaluation‚Äù from each professor).

There are competing explanations for why UCSC was designed, both physically and academically, as it is. One common explanation is that the UC wanted it to be a de-centralized campus to prevent large scale protests and disruptions that were possible on a campus like Berkeley, where the anti-Vietnam student movement was strong. A second explanation is that the UC wished to mimic the Oxford-type college structure, where each student was assigned to a college where faculty also lived, and there was approximately one faculty member per subject area, thereby creating a more interdisciplinary academic community within each college. The goal, as UC President Clark Kerr put it, was to ‚Äúmake the campus seem small as it grows larger‚Äù [citation].

In 1978, Chancellor Sinsheimer wanted to reorganize the university. The colleges evolved from being interdisciplinary to being hubs for faculty in particular disciplines, essentially mimicking the typical ‚Äúdepartment.‚Äù This restructuring continued in 1991, when Chancellor Stevens decreased college funding and increased the importance of individual departments over the interdisciplinary colleges by giving more academic control to the department structures that had formed. In 2000, the Faculty Senate was formed and UCSC officially began using an ABC grading system, ending the narrative evaluation.

UCSC received federal designation as a Hispanic-Serving Institution (HSI) in 2012, at which point it had met the threshold of having greater than 25% of its undergraduate student population be Hispanic/Latinx. This designation gave the university the opportunity to apply for grants to develop programs for Hispanic/Latinx students. Students have reported that these resources are great, but in actuality, student-run organizations have done more to build a feeling of belonging and that work largely goes unrecognized. [citation]

The designation of HSI has allowed schools serving underrepresented populations to gain access to federal grants. A lawsuit borne out of Tennessee seeks to declare the ethnicity requirement as unconstitutional, potentially spelling the end of the program. The Department of Justice has declared they will not defend the legal challenge, so the future of the program is up in the air. [citation]

The University of California System

The University of California is the largest public university system in the country, with ~300,000 students enrolled and 265,000 faculty and staff. There are ten separate campuses: Berkeley, Davis, Irvine, Los Angeles, Merced, Riverside, San Diego, San Francisco, Santa Barbara, and Santa Cruz, and there are three national labs associated with the UC system: Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL), Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL), and Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL).

The highest level of governance is the Board of Regents (sometimes written out as ‚Äúthe Regents of the University of California‚Äù), which works with the state government to maintain and fund the UC system. The Board of Regents has 26 members: 18 members appointed by the Governor of California, 1 student member, and 7 ‚Äúex officio" members (a bunch of state government people, including the Governor, and the UC President). The Regents deal with ‚Äúuniversity policies, financial affairs, tuition and fees‚Äù [citation].

This sounds like a great way to politicize education, but it actually doesn‚Äôt work that way. In the bylaws of the UC system, the Board of Regents is actually not allowed to make basically any academic decisions, they have completely ceded that power to the Academic Senate, which is composed of normal faculty members from all campuses.

The Board of Regents elects the UC President, who then runs the UC Office of the President (UCOP). The UC President manages the overall UC system from an operational perspective (again, not direct management over the academics of each campus). Each campus has their own leadership to manage their own university - these leaders consult with and answer to the UCOP, but there is some separation there. The current UC President, James B. Milliken, is paid a salary of $1,475,000 [citation], but god forbid grad students can pay rent.

Each individual campus is run by its Chancellor and Campus Provost & Executive Vice Chancellor (abbreviated CPEVC or CP/EVC). At UC Santa Cruz, as of August 2025, the Chancellor is Cynthia Larive. We have an interim CPEVC, Paul Koch. The previous CPEVC, Lori Kletzer, was not well-liked by anyone!

In September 2024, the Board of Regents approved salary increases for all 10 chancellors. This brought Cynthia‚Äôs salary to $795,000. Apparently, she declined the increase [citation] - I guess the $642,389 she was already making was enough [citation]. The new UCSC Chancellor‚Äôs salary is >13 times the salary of the highest paid graduate student worker at UCSC today. Lori Kletzer, the former CPEVC, made $504,492 in 2023 [citation].

The CPEVC more directly oversees the academic divisions (and their deans) of the university, including the Physical & Biological Sciences (PBSci) division. Our department is housed under PBSci. The current dean of PBSci is Bryan Gaensler, who has an appointment as a faculty member in Astronomy, as well. Each department has a chair, who attends regular meetings with the dean of their division, alongside the other department chairs from that division. The current chair in Astronomy is Jonathan Fortney. When Jonathan has an issue to raise, he begins with the dean, Bryan Gaensler, who can then decide to raise it further up the chain to the CPEVC or a different office under the CPEVC.

A history of Student Activism

This history is by no means comprehensive!

The first UCSC commencement in 1969 saw students throw their diplomas at UC President Clark Kerr and Chancellor Dean McHenry. Student activists disrupted the ceremony to award imprisoned Black Panther leader Huey P. Newton an Honorary Degree. This took place shortly after the 1969 People's Park protest. [citation]

In the 1970s, UCSC students were active in the labor movement and anti-Vietnam war protests. Students would distribute posters asking people to not purchase lettuce from certain stores when farm workers in Salinas were on strike [citation].

There have been several major protest movements surrounding environmental justice at UCSC - the most recent and notable was in 2007. [story text taken from citation, this protest had a website you can visit here]

In 2003, UCSC developed a plan called the Long Range Development Plan (LRDP) which would destroy 120 acres of redwood forest in the University‚Äôs upper campus and add 4,500 students to the school. On the morning of November 7, 2007, at 1am, twelve students from UCSC and activists from across the state joined together to hoist platforms into second-generation redwood trees where construction of a new biomedical building was slated to begin. Using pulleys and ropes, the activists installed two prebuilt platforms into three clusters of trees. At 11am, 400 people, including students, alumni, and community members, marched to the Tree Sit site. Upon arriving, police pepper sprayed those who tried to get close to the trees that held the sitters and arrested five protestors. Eventually, the marchers pushed the police out of the grove, occupying the space underneath the Tree Sitters. The following day protestors built an encampment under the occupied trees. They set up ziplines between the platforms so that the sitters might be able to move from one tree to the other without touching the ground. Between November 8 and December 13, those living on the ground under the trees held dances, potlucks, movie screenings, classes, and teach-ins for visitors curious about the Tree Sit and the University‚Äôs campus expansion plans. The following day, the University filed a lawsuit against the Tree Sitters and those who supported them, requesting monetary damages. The following month, on January 12, police attacked twenty people gathered below the Tree Sitters. University faculty and staff responded to the violence by requesting that the administration limit the police‚Äôs usage of force on the protestors. Ultimately, the university waited-out the Tree Sitters for over a year. When other lawsuits failed, the Tree Sitters decided to end their protest in December 2008.
